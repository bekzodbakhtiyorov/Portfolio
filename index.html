Professional Persona Evolution

Identity Meets Reputation

Throughout this semester, through the Radian platform and my BCOM 314 coursework at the Eller College of Management, I collected and analyzed data that revealed how I see myself versus how others perceive me. Here is the story of my professional growth.

My Communication Identity: The Amiable

The Self-Perception Inventory (SPI) — a behavioral communication assessment — placed me squarely in the Amiable quadrant: people-oriented, a bridge-builder, and deeply motivated by harmony and consensus. As an Amiable, I'm often the glue holding teams together, attuned to emotions, and driven by genuine social connection.

Interestingly, my five PRS (Professional Reputation Survey) respondents collectively perceived me as Expressive — energetic, outgoing, and idea-oriented. This gap reveals something important: while I internally value steadiness and people harmony, those around me experience me as dynamic and forward-thinking. This alignment in warmth, combined with a difference in expressiveness, gives me a unique professional edge — I can be both the anchor and the spark.

Where I Thrive: Work Environment Preferences

The Work Environment Preferences (WEP) dimensions from my SPI reveal that I prefer direct, low-context communication, consensus-based decision-making, and environments where feedback is valued but delivered with care.

My standout scores: Feedback (2.33) and Relationships (2.33) point to a preference for cultures where trust is built through both work and social interaction. My low Context score (0.67) reflects my preference for clear, explicit messaging — a trait that aligns naturally with finance and data analysis environments.

My strong motivators — Social Interest, Financial Interest, and Decision-Making Interest — align perfectly with a career in wealth management, where client relationships, financial outcomes, and data-driven decisions converge.

Solo Work — Written Project

Regenerative Agriculture in Uzbekistan

Context: Created independently during my internship at the Ministry of Economy and Finance of Uzbekistan in Summer 2025. This policy analysis was submitted as a formal research brief to support government-level agricultural reform discussions.

What I Did

I authored this comprehensive 2027–2032 development outlook entirely on my own — conducting original research, building data models, and writing all policy recommendations. The report covers agricultural production dynamics, land and water resource challenges, and lessons from Australia, Brazil, and the USA.

Skills Demonstrated

  • 📊 Quantitative data analysis from national statistical bodies (Uzbekistan Statistics Agency, FAO)
  • 📈 Economic modeling — production volume forecasts, growth trend analysis
  • 🌍 Comparative international policy research (3 countries)
  • ✍️ Professional policy writing in Uzbek (native language)
  • 📋 Government-level report formatting and policy recommendation design

Key Findings

  • Uzbekistan's agricultural output grew 1.78× between 2020–2024 (250T to 444.6T som)
  • 65% of land (28M hectares) experiencing various degrees of degradation
  • Australia reduced chemical fertilizer use by 66–81% via mixed cropping — directly applicable to Uzbekistan
  • Proposed a 2027–2032 roadmap for regenerative agriculture adoption at national scale
Note: This project was completed entirely by me independently during my internship at the Ministry of Finance of Uzbekistan — a real professional deliverable, not a class assignment.
